Sequoia [U.S.A]

Sequoia was the world’s most powerful supercomputer back in June 2012 but has been sliding down the Top500 list ever since. By November 2017 it had slipped to 6th place. Like Cori and Trinity, Sequoia belongs to the U.S. Department of Energy, sitting in the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory. Unlike them, though, Sequoia was built by IBM and uses that company’s 16-core Power BQC chips with a custom interconnect. It has 98,304 processors for a total of 1,572,864 cores, each with 1 gigabyte of RAM. Together, they deliver a maximum sustained performance of 17.17 petaflops and a peak performance of 20.13 petaflops. When it comes to energy efficiency, the Power chips Sequoia uses are nothing spectacular, delivering a middling 2.177 gigaflops/watt.
